Water Quality Matters

Tap water can be hard and alter flavor. Use bottled or filtered water at 90-95°C (just off boil). Let boiled water sit 30 seconds before using. The grind should feel like "fine sand" — too fine and pressure stalls; too coarse and the shot is weak.

Five Steps to Perfection

Follow these steps precisely. The OneTwoCups requires firm, steady pressure to reach its 20-bar potential.

Step 01
Prep the Filter
Insert the ground coffee filter. Add 18-20g of medium-fine coffee. Level the bed with your finger — don't tamp hard, just settle evenly.
Step 02
Heat & Add Water
Pour 60-80ml of hot water (90-95°C) into the reservoir. This is a "ristretto" style short shot — concentrated and bold. Screw the top on firmly.
Step 03
Press with Power
Hold over your cup. Press down firmly with both thumbs. Feel strong resistance — that's 20 bars building. Extract for 20-30 seconds until the stream blondes.
Step 04
Ice Preparation
Fill a tall glass with 150g of large ice cubes. Big cubes melt slower. Pro move: make coffee ice cubes from yesterday's brew.
Step 05
The Pour & Layer
Pour hot espresso directly over ice. Watch the crema separate and float. Add 60-80ml cold water to complete the "long black" style.

The "Freddo" Variation

For extra creaminess: Pour hot espresso into a cocktail shaker with 2 ice cubes. Shake vigorously for 10 seconds. Strain over fresh ice. This creates a thick, velvety foam layer that transforms the drink entirely.

Why Salt in Coffee?

"Salt doesn't just mask bitterness — it blocks it at the receptor level, allowing hidden sweetness and complexity to emerge."

Adding salt to coffee is not a gimmick. Sodium ions (Na+) bind to bitter taste receptors on your tongue, physically preventing bitter compounds from activating them. This is called cross-modal perception.

🔬

Bitter Blocker

Sodium ions occupy bitter receptors, reducing harshness from chlorogenic acids and phenylindanes in dark roasts.

🍯

Sweetness Amplifier

By suppressing bitterness, your palate perceives the coffee's natural sugars more vividly and completely.

⚡

Electrolyte Balance

Caffeine causes mild sodium loss. A pinch replaces this, preventing the "wired but tired" afternoon crash.

The darker the roast, the more bitter compounds (phenylindanes) are present. This makes salted iced black coffee particularly effective — the cold temperature already reduces perceived bitterness, and salt completes the smoothing effect.

Historical Context

Scandinavian sailors brewed coffee with seawater when fresh water was scarce. Nordic countries still serve salt alongside sugar at coffee houses. In Turkey, brides-to-be traditionally serve salted coffee as a premarital ceremony — a test of character.

Which Salt to Use?

Not all salts are equal. Mineral content affects flavor integration and mouthfeel.

🏆

Fine Sea Salt

Clean, neutral flavor. Dissolves instantly in hot coffee. No additives or anti-caking agents. Trace minerals enhance complexity. The best choice.

🌸

Pink Himalayan

84 trace minerals. Slightly earthy undertone. Beautiful with Indonesian/Sumatra beans. Use sparingly — stronger flavor profile.

🧂

Kosher Salt

Large flakes — crush first. Pure sodium chloride. Good for pre-ground mixing. Harder to dissolve in cold liquid.

❌

Iodized Table Salt

Iodine adds metallic taste. Anti-caking agents affect mouthfeel. Can clash with coffee aromatics. Creates chemical aftertaste. Avoid.

When to Salt, When to Skip

✓

Do Salt

  • Dark roasts
  • Robusta blends
  • Over-extracted shots
  • Office / diner coffee
  • Hard water areas
  • Fasting mornings
✗

Skip Salt

  • Light single-origins
  • Yemen / Gesha / Panama
  • Fruity naturals
  • Floral coffees
  • Delicate acidity stars
  • First cup of the day

The "Scott Rao" Standard

Coffee roasting expert Scott Rao found that 0.15g of salt per 100g of brewed coffee yielded the best results. For your OneTwoCups double shot (60ml ≈ 60g), that's roughly 0.09g — barely a dusting on the fingertip.
