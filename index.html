Introduction

As a cook, tastes and smells are my memories. And now I'm in search of new ones. So I'm leaving New York City in hope to have a few epiphanies around the world. And I'm willing to go to some lengths to do that. I'm looking for extremes of emotion and experience. I'll try anything, I'll risk everything, I have nothing to lose.

My fellow Americans, what is our one culinary contribution to the world that's as original as jazz, baseball, rock and roll? That's right, barbecue. Ribs fall right off the bone. It's all ours. Even the French can't do it.

What do I know about barbecue? Yep, that about sums it up for me. I kid, I kid. Actually, it turns out there's a barbecue debate more heated than the last presidential election. There is just no element of barbecue that's not controversial. The cooking unit, the meat, fuel, the seasoning, and the most elusive, the expertise of the chef.

Barbecue is not a cuisine, it's an obsession. Barbecuers are free-thinking individualists.

— Anthony Bourdain

This booklet collects the recipes, techniques, and wisdom gathered from three corners of the BBQ Triangle — Kansas City, Texas, and North Carolina — along with the real places you can still visit today.

The Carolina Pork Sandwich

What You Need
  • 4–5 lb pork shoulder (Boston butt)
  • Yellow mustard (as binder)
  • KC-style rub (see Paul Kirk's recipe below)
  • Soft white buns
  • Coleslaw (vinegar-based)
  • Bubba sauce or your favorite sweet & tangy BBQ sauce
The Method
  1. Coat pork shoulder with yellow mustard, then apply rub liberally.
  2. Smoke at 225°F for 10–12 hours until internal temp reaches 195–205°F.
  3. Rest 30 minutes, then pull/shred with two forks.
  4. Toss pulled pork with a little sauce — don't drown it.
  5. Pile on a soft bun, top with vinegar slaw, drizzle with more sauce.
Note: Good barbecue is not always pretty. Don't worry about Instagram-perfect — worry about flavor.

Paul Kirk's Basic Rub

Foundation (The Four Salts)
  • 2 tbsp seasoning salt
  • 1 tbsp celery salt
  • 1 tbsp onion salt
  • 1 tbsp garlic salt
Add Your Signature — Pick Three
  • Coriander
  • Oregano
  • Cumin
  • Allspice
  • Paprika
  • Brown sugar
Paul's Pro Tip: "Make it your own." The basic four-salt rub is just the foundation. Add three signature spices to develop your personal flavor profile.

Burnt Ends — The KC Treasure

The Method
  1. Start with a full packer brisket (point end). Trim fat cap to ¼ inch.
  2. Apply rub generously. Let sit overnight in fridge.
  3. Smoke at 225°F using hickory or oak until internal temp hits 195°F (8–10 hours).
  4. Separate the point from the flat. Cube the point into 1-inch chunks.
  5. Toss cubes in a pan with BBQ sauce + a splash of cola or apple juice.
  6. Return to smoker for 1–2 more hours until sticky and caramelized.
Note: A burnt end is the charred part of the brisket after it's cooked that they used to throw away. Only in Kansas City did they really become menu items.

Dave Klose's Signature Shrimp

Ingredients
  • 8–10 count tiger shrimp (jumbo), peeled and deveined
  • Stone crab claw meat
  • Chicken tenders, pounded thin
  • Maple-smoked bacon
  • Cabernet Sauvignon
  • Toothpicks or butcher's twine
The Method
  1. Butterfly each shrimp, leaving tail on.
  2. Stuff each shrimp with stone crab meat.
  3. Wrap stuffed shrimp with a pounded-thin chicken tender.
  4. Wrap the entire bundle with maple-smoked bacon. Secure with toothpicks.
  5. Soak in Cabernet Sauvignon overnight (or at least 4 hours).
  6. Smoke or grill at 275°F until bacon is crispy and chicken is cooked through (about 45 min).
Dave's Wisdom: "You cannot mess this up. If you do it bad, it's incredible. If you do it right, it'll take the top of your head off."

Texas Post Oak Brisket

The Texas Trinity Rub
  • 2.5 tbsp kosher salt
  • 2.5 tbsp coarse black pepper
  • 2 tbsp garlic powder (optional)
  • Post oak wood chunks or chips
  • Beef tallow (for wrapping)
The Method
  1. Trim brisket: remove silver skin, square off sides, leave ¼ inch fat cap.
  2. Season with salt & pepper only. Dry brine overnight in fridge.
  3. Preheat smoker to 250–265°F. Add post oak for smoke.
  4. Place brisket fat-side toward heat. Smoke 7–8 hours until 165–170°F internal.
  5. Wrap in butcher paper drizzled with beef tallow.
  6. Continue smoking 7–8 more hours until 202–203°F internal.
  7. Rest at room temperature at least 2 hours before slicing.
Why Post Oak? Post oak burns slow and steady with a mild, clean smoke that enhances beef without overpowering it. It's the gold standard in Texas BBQ.

Eastern NC Whole Hog (Home Adaptation)

"Whole Hog" Assembly for the Home Cook
  • 2–3 lb pork shoulder (Boston butt)
  • 1 lb pork belly
  • 1 lb pork tenderloin
  • 1 rack baby back ribs
  • Kosher salt, black pepper, smoked paprika
  • Apple cider vinegar (for sauce)
The Method
  1. Cut all pork pieces in half. Season with salt, pepper, and smoked paprika.
  2. Layer in a Dutch oven: ribs on bottom, then shoulder, tenderloin, belly, sirloin, top with remaining ribs.
  3. Wrap tightly in 3–4 layers of heavy-duty foil.
  4. Roast at 250°F for 8–10 hours until falling-off-bone tender.
  5. Remove bones, pull and shred all meat together. Mix in rendered fat.
  6. Toss with Eastern NC vinegar sauce. Top with crispy skin cracklins if available.
Ed's Philosophy: The blend of shoulder, belly, and tenderloin mimics whole-hog flavor. If you kill it, use it all.

The Sauce Wars

Just when I think my investigation is over, I discover barbecue has battles within battles. Turns out in North Carolina, there's a barbecue civil war.

When you're talking about barbecue in North Carolina, you're talking about two major significant styles. You got your East North Carolina barbecue, which is kind of a vinegary whole hog thing, and you got your western, or Piedmont-Lexington style barbecue.

Traditionally, the people east of Carolina thought tomatoes were poisonous. As they came west, they began to add tomatoes.

Eastern NC Style
No Tomato Allowed
  • 1 cup apple cider vinegar
  • 1 cup white distilled vinegar
  • 1 tbsp crushed red pepper flakes
  • 1 tsp freshly ground black pepper
  • 1 tsp kosher salt
  • 1–2 tsp sugar (optional)
  • Dash of hot sauce
Shake all ingredients in a jar. Let stand 30 minutes. Refrigerate 1–2 days for best flavor.
Western NC Style
Lexington Dip
  • ¾ cup apple cider vinegar
  • ½ cup ketchup
  • 1–2 tbsp brown sugar
  • 1 tbsp Worcestershire sauce
  • 1 tsp crushed red pepper
  • 1 tsp black pepper
  • ½ tsp salt
Combine and simmer 10 minutes. Or shake cold and let rest overnight.

We were barbecuing when those people in Texas were chasing cows.

— Bill Leeson & Jim Tabb, Western NC

Bill & Jim — Western NC Style

Representing the west side are Bill Leeson and Jim Tabb. Even in a real war, these guys would be useful. Jim is an ex-pilot, and Bill is an expert at blowing stuff up. But in the barbecue war, they're generals.

Strategy number one, moist shoulder meat. Shoulder is a juicy piece of meat. It's got more fat in the shoulder than the ham. Strategy number two? Direct heat. Far as we're concerned, direct heat is the way to cook.

Instead of a separate fire box, these guys cook right on top of the flame. Anywhere from 12 to 14 hours. That smoke's rolling now. Can someone hand me a knife? I know we won't need a knife. I'm furious if we need a knife.

Pulled Pork Shoulder (Western NC)

What You Need
  • 8–10 lb pork shoulder (bone-in preferred)
  • Kosher salt & black pepper
  • Apple cider vinegar (for mopping)
  • Red slaw & hush puppies (for serving)
  • Western NC sauce (Lexington Dip)
The Method
  1. Season shoulder generously with salt and pepper. Let sit 1 hour.
  2. Set up smoker for indirect heat at 225°F. Add hickory or oak.
  3. Place shoulder fat-side up. Smoke 12–14 hours, mopping with vinegar every 2 hours.
  4. Target internal temp: 195–205°F. Probe should slide in like butter.
  5. Rest 1 hour. Pull apart with forks or gloved hands — no knife needed.
  6. Serve with red slaw, hush puppies, and Lexington Dip on the side.
Bill's Test: "If you need a knife, you got roast pork." The meat should pull apart effortlessly.

Backyard Coca-Cola Ribs
What You Need
  • 2 racks baby back ribs
  • Yellow mustard (as binder)
  • Your favorite BBQ rub
  • 1 cup Coca-Cola
  • ½ cup brown sugar
  • Beer (for the pitmaster)
  • Salad (optional, mostly forgotten)
The Method
  1. Remove membrane from back of ribs. Coat with mustard, then rub.
  2. Smoke at 225°F for 3 hours.
  3. Mix Coca-Cola and brown sugar into a slurry.
  4. Wrap ribs in foil, pour slurry over, seal tightly.
  5. Return to smoker for 1 more hour.
  6. Unwrap, sauce if desired, smoke 30 min to set glaze.
  7. Rest 10 minutes. The meat should literally fall off the bone.
The Essence: "Sitting in the backyard, waiting for the ribs to cook, drinking beer. This is the barbecue story encapsulated."

Pitmaster Wisdom

Eight Lessons from the Smoke

21

Lessons from the Fire

I
Patience is the Secret Ingredient

Every pitmaster in the episode cooks 8–16 hours. Brisket needs 14+. There is no shortcut. "It's the absolute antithesis of fast food."

II
Wood Matters More Than Sauce

Post oak in Texas. Hickory and oak in KC. Oak and hickory in NC. The fuel defines the flavor. Roy Burns: "Post oak, that's the secret doing the slow."

III
Make Your Rub Your Own

Paul Kirk's foundation is four salts. Then add three signature spices. "Make it your own" was his signature advice to every student.

IV
Nose to Tail, Everything But the Squeak

Ed Mitchell's philosophy: if you kill it, use it all. Trotters, ears, tails, chitlins, cracklins — every part has a place at the pig bar.

V
The Backyard is Sacred

John Lonergan's method: mustard rub, 4 hours, Coca-Cola & brown sugar slurry, beer in hand. "The essence of barbecue is sitting in the backyard, waiting."

VI
If You Need a Knife, It's Wrong

Bill Leeson's test: properly cooked pulled pork falls apart with no knife. "If you need a knife, you got roast pork." Probe should slide like butter.

VII
Sauce is a Civil War

Eastern NC: vinegar only, no tomato ("tomatoes are poisonous"). Western NC: a little ketchup. Kansas City: thick & sweet. Texas: often no sauce at all.

VIII
Barbecue is an Endless Learning Process

Even the masters admit they don't know it all. Every cook teaches something new. The journey is the destination — there is no "perfect" barbecue.
