BBQ Quest
01 — THE BARBECUE CAPITAL
🔥

Kansas City

"If it moves, barbecue it."

Kansas City is a microcosm of the barbecue world. While other regions specialize in specific meats, in KC they'll cook anything, anyhow. This is where burnt ends — once discarded — became menu royalty.

"Good barbecue is not always pretty. It's the absolute antithesis of fast food."

— Anthony Bourdain

Gas Station Institution

Oklahoma Joe's

Located inside a functioning gas station, liquor store, and convenience store. Some of the finest barbecue in the country, hiding in plain sight.

Signature Dishes
Carolina Pork Sandwich (pulled pork with slaw + Bubba sauce), Burnt Ends, Ribs

"If you want to get to the bottom of something, you start at the top."

Carolyn Wells (KC Barbecue Society) recommends the Carolina Pork Sandwich and notes that burnt ends are collected all week to be offered one night. The ribs are always great. Bourdain describes the flavor as "up the ying-yang."

Grand Master / Trainer

Paul Kirk — Baron of Barbecue

World-recognized authority on Kansas City barbecue. A competition prize fighter turned manager who now trains rookies in the sacred arts of the pit.

Signature Method
14-hour brisket, custom rubs (seasoning/celery/onion/garlic salt + signature spices), $8,000 custom Klose pit

"I'm searching for that ultimate barbecue. I've never found it."

Bourdain gets a hands-on lesson trimming brisket (rookie status confirmed). The basic rub is built on four salts, then customized with signature spices like coriander, oregano, cumin, or allspice. The 14-hour cook time produces a beautiful smoke ring and moist, juicy meat.

KC Techniques & Secrets
Burnt ends — the charred brisket pieces once thrown away, now the most prized menu item
Basic rub foundation: seasoning salt, celery salt, onion salt, garlic salt
14+ hour cook time for certified Angus brisket — low and slow is the only way
Custom pits by Klose Pits in Texas — $8,000+ of pure craftsmanship
Complete with "barbecue nerds" — plastic pocket protectors and meat thermometers included
02 — THE PIT BUILDERS
🤠

Texas

"Nothing beats Texas-style barbecue."

Texans don't just make barbecue — they engineer it. From bullet-proof glass pits to 14-foot beer bottle smokers, the pit is as important as the meat. Post oak is the secret fuel that makes it all happen.

"It's not just food, it's not just a lifestyle, it's a calling... it's an illness."

— Dave Klose

Pit Engineer / Mad Scientist

Dave Klose — Klose Pits

Houston's legendary pit builder. Custom designs for every need — from NASA administrators to hungry cosmonauts. If you can dream it, Dave can weld it.

Signature Creations
Bullet-proof Sarajevo phone booth pits, 14-foot beer bottle smokers with pony keg taps, car pits, .357 Magnum-tested steel

"I won't stop until I've made something out of everything on planet Earth."

Dave worked at NASA and once asked the administrator for a pit on the moon — "so when the Russians land, they'll see the world's first Klose interplanetary grill." His signature dish: 8-10 count tiger shrimp stuffed with stone crab, wrapped in maple-smoked bacon, soaked in Cabernet Sauvignon overnight. "If you do it bad, it's incredible. If you do it right, it'll take the top of your head off."

30-Year Institution

Roy Burns BBQ

An unassuming Houston joint where limos and Cadillacs line up alongside pickup trucks. The building looks like a decent sneeze would level it — but the barbecue is bulletproof.

Signature
Ribs and brisket with secret sauce, post oak fuel, mustard-based potato salad with pickles

"Post oak, that's the secret doing the slow."

Roy's secret is slow-burning post oak. The ribs cook for 5-6 more hours after Bourdain's visit. The sauce is so secret Roy wouldn't give it up "if you put a gun to his head." His philosophy: "I tell my customers to come back and tell me if it's bad or good. Then I start looking for ways to change." The asymmetric flavors of mustard potato salad with pickles perfectly complement the meat.

Texas Techniques & Secrets
Post oak as the secret fuel — slow burning, consistent heat, unmistakable flavor
Pit engineering as an art form — custom designed for individual needs
Direct heat methods — cooking right on top of the flame
12-14 hour cook times — patience is the ultimate ingredient
Sausage-stuffed, bacon-wrapped quail — because everything is bigger (and wrapped in bacon) in Texas
03 — THE CRADLE OF BARBECUE
🐷

North Carolina

"Where most people will tell you it all began."

It's all about pig. Whole hog. Nose to tail. Everything but the squeak. And within NC, a civil war rages — Eastern (vinegar, whole hog) vs. Western/Piedmont (tomato, shoulder only).

"If you're gonna kill a pig, you should eat every part of it. If the pig ever hunts me down and kills me, I hope that he uses every part."

— Ed Mitchell

Eastern NC — Whole Hog

Mitchell's — Ed Mitchell

The temple of whole hog barbecue. Ed Mitchell takes you from live pig to pig bar, with every part honored — trotters, tails, ears, chitlins, and the legendary crispy skin cracklins.

Signature
Whole hog 8-10 hours, hand-chopped, vinegar-pepper sauce, crispy cracklins, the famous "pig bar"

"This is good eating. Now she's ready to go on. Spank it."

Bourdain helps trim the pig (feet, fat glands) before it hits the pit for 8-10 hours. The pit crew debones, pulls ribs, and hand-chops everything. The sauce is pure Eastern NC: red & black pepper, sugar, salt, and vinegar — no tomato. Ed taste-tests for tenderness before signing off. The pig bar features trotters, tails, ears, and chitlins. "Best pig foot I've ever had. And I've had a fair amount of them."

Western NC — Piedmont/Lexington Style

Bill Leeson & Jim Tabb

Generals in the NC barbecue civil war. Jim is an ex-pilot, Bill is an explosives expert. Together they defend the western style: pork shoulder, direct heat, and yes — tomatoes in the sauce.

Signature
Pulled shoulder, direct heat (cooked right on the flame), 12-14 hours, tomato-tinged sauce, red slaw, hush puppies

"We were barbecuing when those people in Texas were chasing cows."

Western NC uses shoulder only (juicier, more fat than ham) cooked with direct heat right on top of the flame. The sauce includes tomato — "traditionally, people east of Carolina thought tomatoes were poisonous. As they came west, they began to add tomatoes." The pulled pork comes off the bone with no knife needed. Served with red slaw and hush puppies. Bill is "furious if we need a knife."

NC Techniques & Secrets
Eastern NC: Whole hog, vinegar & pepper sauce — absolutely no tomato
Western NC: Pork shoulder only, direct heat, tomato-acceptable sauce
Sauce formula: red & black pepper + lots of sugar + even more salt + real lot of vinegar
Crispy skin cracklins sprinkled on top — "all the things I like about food, all in one dish"
The pig bar: trotters, tails, ears, chitlins — "if there's a buffet in heaven, it looks pretty much like that"
8-14 hour cook times depending on style — whole hog needs less time than shoulder

The Backyard Experience

Most of America doesn't get their barbecue from award-winning pit masters or famous joints. They get it from their own backyard. This is where the real magic happens.

"This is the essence of barbecue. Sitting in the backyard, waiting for the ribs to cook, drinking beer. This is the barbecue story encapsulated."

— John Lonergan's Backyard
