The World Cup Analogy

Every four years, the world watches the FIFA World Cup. Our eyes are drawn to the pitch — the star players, the spectacular goals, the trophy lift. Yet beneath every victory lies a deeper architecture: strategy, team building, governance, and years of disciplined process.

Business and economics ask the very same questions. What can be won? What strategy wins the match? Football is not merely art and passion; it is system, discipline, and relentless competition.

In this exclusive conversation, Dr. Khatib Basri — former Minister of Finance of Indonesia (2013–2014) and widely regarded as the nation's finest economic storyteller — draws a sweeping parallel between the beautiful game and the hard economics of national development.

"What happens on the field is only the final act. The real drama is in the decades of preparation, the systems built, and the talent cultivated long before the whistle blows."

The interview explores why Indonesia, despite vast potential, remains trapped in a defensive posture — and what it must learn from Vietnam, Germany, Argentina, and the relentless logic of global competition.

2
A Love Affair with Football

Dr. Basri grew up in the 1970s, an era without television, satellite dishes, or the internet. To learn the result of a Netherlands vs. Germany match, he had to wait for the morning newspaper — Kompas — which arrived the next day, even though the match had been played the night before.

Live broadcasts were therefore extraordinary events. He still remembers the rare privilege of bringing a television to school to watch morning matches with his classmates.

It was the era of Total Football — Rinus Michels, Johan Cruyff, Wim van Hanegem, Johnny Rep. Germany had Franz Beckenbauer. But the 1978 World Cup changed everything: Basri fell in love with Mario Kempes, Osvaldo Ardiles, and the Argentine spirit. He has supported Argentina ever since — through the Maradona era of 1986 to the Messi era of today. He wears the jersey still.

As a boy, he played for MBFA (Merdeka Boys Football Association) at Lapangan Banteng. Football boots had nails on the soles, not plastic studs, bought at Senen market from a shop called Shong Fu. The coolest position was goalkeeper — anak gawang. He played striker, then gradually dropped to defender and midfielder as his stamina faded.

For the 2026 World Cup, he follows Argentina closely — Emiliano Martínez, Messi, Cristian Romero. His heart says Argentina; his head says Spain. And yes, he heard about Messi's hattrick this morning.

3
The Envelope from Wijoyo

Around 2002–2006, Dr. Basri received a brown envelope addressed to "The Honorable Khatib Basri." The sender was Wijoyo Nitiastro — a legendary economic figure. Basri assumed it was serious official correspondence.

He opened it. Inside was a football match schedule — complete with Wijoyo's typed team analysis. The message read: "Don't work too hard. Enjoy football."

But one phrase struck Basri permanently: "If you can't beat them, join them." Wijoyo was writing about naturalized French players on the national team. His strategic point: when you cannot dominate an opponent through brute force, you adapt — you integrate their strengths into your own system.

Wijoyo was not merely talking about football. He was talking about agility — the capacity to read the field, recognize superior force, and adjust your formation. In business and economics, this is not surrender; it is strategic realism.

The core insight: The greatest threat to a nation or a firm is not a stronger competitor. It is the inability to adjust when the terrain shifts.

This single envelope, sent by a senior statesman who understood that economics and football share the same grammar, became a lens through which Basri has viewed Indonesian development ever since.

4
Agility & The Dinosaur

Dr. Basri often returns to a scene from Jurassic Park. The dinosaurs were the largest, most powerful creatures to ever walk the earth. They ruled for millennia. And then they were gone.

The lesson is brutal and clear: survival is not about being the strongest or the smartest. It is about being the most capable of adjustment. In an agile world, agility is the master key. Without it, obsolescence is not a risk — it is a certainty.

"The dinosaur was a massive animal, but it went extinct. What determines survival is not who is strongest or smartest, but who can adjust the fastest."

Indonesia's central problem in business and economics is precisely this: an inability to adjust quickly. The decision-making apparatus lacks discipline, transparency, and public interaction. Regulations appear without public awareness, pushed through at lightning speed. Bureaucratic uncertainty is so extreme that, as Basri puts it, "you can do nothing but pray."

Business does not need sermons. It does not need to be taught. It needs certainty — predictable rules, consistent enforcement, and a state that does not change the goalposts overnight. The first duty of government toward the private sector is to create a field where the players can look up and pass, rather than watching their feet for stones.

5
Indonesia as Catenaccio

Asked which football team Indonesia's economy most resembles, Dr. Basri does not hesitate: Italy's Catenaccio. A system built on defense. A team that holds, waits, absorbs pressure, and only throws the ball forward once — hoping for a single lethal counterattack.

Indonesia is inward-looking. It is busy being a firefighter — solving domestic problems, managing internal contradictions, celebrating the size of its domestic market. It is not offensive. It does not play a significant global role. It defends.

Consider Vietnam. A smaller nation, historically devastated, yet now a production-based economy. LG and Samsung manufacture in Vietnam not for the domestic market but for the entire world. Vietnam's market is global. Even under Trump's tariffs, it grew at 8%.

Consider Singapore. A city-state with no hinterland, yet it became a financial hub, a digital hub, and now an AI hub. It had no choice but to be proactive. Indonesia, by contrast, arrives late to every strategic opportunity — complacent in its archipelagic vastness.

⚠️ Indonesia must abandon the Catenaccio mindset. The domestic market is a starting point, not a destination.
6
The Resource Curse

Why is Indonesia defensive? Dr. Basri points to Jeffrey Sachs's "Resource Curse." Nations endowed with abundant natural resources — oil, gas, minerals, fertile land — often fail to develop advanced economies. Of all resource-rich nations, only Canada and Australia have truly advanced. Across Africa, oil wealth has produced stagnation, not prosperity.

In economics, this is also called Dutch Disease — a syndrome where resource wealth kills creativity, hollows out manufacturing, and traps a nation in commodity dependence. The resource is "enough." It breeds complacency. Why evolve when the ground beneath your feet already pays the bills?

Japan provides the counterfactual. It possesses almost no natural resources. It thrives on creativity, complexity, and relentless incremental innovation. Japan had no choice but to become brilliant.

Indonesia's tragedy is that its resources are just sufficient to prevent the urgency that drove Japan, South Korea, or Singapore. The nation never developed the economic complexity — the gradual evolution from simple to sophisticated capabilities — that transforms poor countries into rich ones.

"We are trapped because our resources are enough. We never had to become advanced."
7
Economic Complexity

Dr. Basri recounts a conversation with Ricardo Hausmann of Harvard University. The debate: should Indonesia prioritize manufacturing or services? Hausmann's answer reframed the question entirely.

He pointed to Toyota — originally a textile machinery company that evolved into the world's largest automaker. He pointed to Nokia — born from Norwegian wood-cutting operations that gradually built electronic capabilities until they produced mobile phones. This is economic complexity in action: starting with simple capabilities and compounding them over decades into entirely new industries.

This process has not happened in Indonesia. There is no indigenous trajectory from palm oil to precision engineering, from coal to semiconductors, from textiles to advanced materials. Each sector remains siloed, extracting and exporting raw commodities rather than climbing the value chain.

The reason, Basri argues, is the Resource Curse combined with low productivity. When a nation can survive by digging, it does not learn to build. When a nation can profit by exporting raw nickel, it does not learn to manufacture batteries. The comfort of the present becomes the trap of the future.

Hausmann's insight: Wealth is not about what you have. It is about what you can do — and how many different things you can do that are hard to replicate.
8
Talent vs. System

World Cup winners follow two distinct models. Latin America produces individual brilliance — the Jogo Bonito of Pelé, the divine madness of Maradona, the genius of Messi. It is beautiful, emotional, and unsustainable. Argentina was lucky: after Maradona, they found Messi. But what comes after Messi?

Germany offers the opposite model. No single German team dominates the global imagination like Brazil 1970 or Argentina 1986. Yet Germany is always in contention. Their 1974 squad — Müller, Beckenbauer, Netzer — was not glamorous, but it was machine-like. Every corner kick, every free kick, every defensive transition was rehearsed, systematic, a set piece.

Indonesia's crisis is that it is neither Germany nor Latin America. The system is weak. Individual talents are scarce. As Basri's friend Yanwar Nugroho wrote in Kompas: the nation should worry about the weakening of technocracy.

Indonesia has a long tradition of technocratic excellence in economic policy. But meritocracy is blocked. Clientelism, political patronage, and inward-looking networks prevent the best from rising. The talent is there — but the system will not let it breathe.

9
Human Capital in the AI Era

Dr. Basri is tired of clichés. He is tired of saying Indonesia has potential. The truth is simpler and harder: Indonesians are already smart. Astra scholars at Oxford perform brilliantly. Indonesian researchers in Japan publish world-class work. Badminton dominance proves the raw capability is real.

The problem is the system prevents talent from flourishing. Meritocracy does not function. The best engineers, scientists, and managers are filtered out by networks rather than nurtured by institutions.

Now comes the AI era. Basri offers a crucial correction to the popular narrative: jobs will not disappear. Jobs will be dominated by those who possess the skills to operate intelligent machines. This is not unemployment. It is skill-biased technological substitution.

"Nurses and doctors will still exist. But which nurses? The ones who can operate robots and AI. Which doctors? The ones who can command diagnostic machines. Surgery may no longer use hands — it will use interfaces."

Without a radical upgrade in human capital quality and without a functioning meritocracy, Indonesia will lose this competition before it even understands the rules. The nation must build German-like systems and let Latin American-style individual brilliance shine.

10
Quick Wins — China Plus One

The United States and China are locked in strategic rivalry. One consequence: Chinese companies increasingly cannot access the American market from mainland China. They must relocate. This is the "China Plus One" phenomenon — and it represents a historic window.

Who is capturing this window? Vietnam. Singapore. Indonesia is capturing remarkably little. The reason is not geography. It is not wages. It is bureaucratic complexity and uncertainty.

"When people deal with Indonesian bureaucracy, they can do nothing but pray — because the uncertainty is that extreme. People become religious out of desperation."

The solution requires zero fiscal stimulus. It is pure deregulation. Make processes easy. Make licensing transparent and fast. One reform already moving in the right direction: PP 28 from BKPM, which states that if a technical ministry fails to respond within a set period, the license is automatically approved. This creates positive administrative silence — a powerful tool for certainty.

Basri's core message to government: "Business does not need to be taught. Just don't disturb it." The state's job is to build the field, paint the lines, and enforce the rules — then get off the pitch.

11
The Amazon Inspiration

During his time at BKPM (Indonesia's investment coordinating board), Dr. Basri faced a constant complaint: investors had no idea where their permit applications sat. A document could disappear into a ministry for weeks, and the applicant had no visibility, no recourse, no certainty.

Basri found inspiration in an unexpected place: Amazon package tracking. He asked his team: "Can we build an intranet for permits where the applicant knows exactly which desk their document is sitting on?"

The psychological effect is profound. Even if the problem is unsolved, knowing "it has been two days at this desk" creates a sense of control. Basri even published his own email address for complaints. He appeared impossibly busy, but in reality he was simply forwarding messages to the relevant deputy with one question: "How many days has this been on your desk?"

Certainty is the cheapest stimulus government can provide. It costs no fiscal outlay. It requires only political will and administrative redesign.

The next stage is human capital development. In the 1980s, most of Indonesia's top bankers were graduates of Citibank's training programs — not government academies. Government education often uses outdated tools (Basri jokes: WordStar, Lotus 1-2-3, floppy disks). Companies, by contrast, use live technology.

The policy lever: double tax deductions for companies that conduct serious employee training. Want better journalists? Let Kompas train them, subsidized by tax policy. Want better engineers? Let industry build the academies.

12
Three Pillars for 2045

Dr. Basri uses a football analogy from PSSI (the Indonesian Football Association). A coach observed that Indonesian players look down at the ball, never seeing their teammates. The reason? The field is full of stones and holes. They must watch their feet to survive. Bad infrastructure creates bad habits.

If the field were safe, players would look up. They would pass. They would see the game. The same is true of national development. Indonesia needs three pillars to reach its 2045 "Golden Indonesia" vision:

1
Infrastructure (Hardware)
Roads, ports, digital backbone, reliable electricity. The physical field on which the economy plays.
2
Human Capital
Training, meritocratic competition, and relentless upskilling. The players on the field.
3
Governance (Software)
Disciplined process, predictable rules, and systematic execution. The playbook and the referee.

Germany's system is sometimes boring. People dislike its rigidity. But governance is what allows a nation to execute set pieces with machine precision — in football and in fiscal policy.

13
The Aging Population Bomb

Without acceleration on all three pillars, Indonesia faces a demographic time bomb. By 2050, the nation will enter an aging population phase. The dependency ratio will shift. The dividend of youth will expire.

The risk is not a financial crisis. The risk is subtler and more permanent: growing old before growing rich. Basri phrases it in stark Indonesian: "Tua sebelum kaya."

A nation that does not build productivity, infrastructure, and governance during its demographic window will find itself with an elderly population and no accumulated wealth to support them. This is the middle-income trap — not a dramatic collapse, but a slow, grinding stagnation.

⚠️ The long-term economic World Cup requires all three pillars. Missing even one risks elimination before the knockout stage.

The trajectory is not predestined. But it is constrained. Every year of delay in reform, every year of bureaucratic inertia, every year of inward-looking policy narrows the window further. The match is already in progress.

14
Message to the Youth

If he were manager of the Indonesian national team — or the Indonesian economy — Dr. Basri's first message would be to the youth. Enthusiasm is fuel. Ambition is not a dirty word. In Indonesia, Basri notes, dreaming big is sometimes viewed with suspicion, as if realism were the only virtue.

"Dream as high as possible. If you fall, you still land relatively high."
— Nelson Mandela: "If your plan does not scare you, it is not ambitious enough."

At Harvard, Basri observes students who dismiss their own chances before even applying. "I could never get in there." His response: try anyway. There is no cost to attempting liberation.

The unique advantage of youth is cognitive audacity. Older generations are "too realistic." They have learned what is "impossible." Young people have not yet absorbed these constraints. They attempt the impossible — and that is precisely how civilization advances.

"Civilization is built by unreasonable people." The reasonable adapt to the world. The unreasonable adapt the world to themselves.

The national team needs fire — the burning desire of young talent. It needs systematic discipline. And it needs meritocracy to identify and elevate the best, regardless of origin or connection.

15
Competition & The Home Champion

Indonesia suffers from a cultural tendency Basri calls "jago kandang" — the home champion. We dominate our own courtyard. We avoid genuine external competition. We build local empires and mistake them for global strength.

This is fatal. Football is competition. Economics is competition. You cannot become a world-class player by practicing only against your cousins. You must face stronger opponents, lose, adapt, and return. The German national team does not train against village clubs. They seek the hardest possible opposition.

Indonesia must embrace relentless external competition — in trade, in investment attraction, in education, in sports. The comfort of domestic monopoly produces brittle champions who collapse on the international stage.

"You cannot become a great player without facing competition from outside. Football is about competition. So is economics."

The prescription is systematic: compete often, at every level, against the best. Let meritocracy — not patronage — determine who wears the national jersey. And build the infrastructure, human capital, and governance that allow the team to look up and pass, rather than staring at their own feet.

16
Message to Business

The single greatest issue facing Indonesian business is uncertainty. Not taxes. Not labor costs. Not infrastructure gaps. Uncertainty.

Business can solve problems. Business can price in costs. What business cannot tolerate is unpredictability. A problem that is known and measurable can be managed. A problem that shifts shape every month destroys planning, investment, and confidence.

Basri offers a simple analogy. You are at the airport. Your flight is delayed two hours. You are annoyed, but you can manage. You buy coffee, make calls, adjust your schedule. Now imagine the airline says: "We don't know when this plane will leave. Maybe two hours. Maybe six. Maybe tomorrow." The uncertainty is paralyzing. You cannot plan. You cannot relax. You can only wait in suspended anxiety.

This is Indonesian bureaucracy. The investor does not know which desk holds their permit, how long it will sit there, or what new regulation will appear next week.

The government's role is to create certainty. Consistent regulations. Simple licensing. Predictable enforcement. When uncertainty becomes intolerable, business gives up. And when business gives up, jobs, taxes, and futures disappear.

Rapid Fire — Part I
▸ Most admired economy: Vietnam. In 1975, Vietnam had just emerged from war while Indonesia was already developing. Today, Vietnam's acceleration is extraordinary — reminiscent of China in the late 1970s. It is a frontier market likely to become emerging. If that happens, Asian stock capital will migrate. A direct threat to Indonesia.
▸ Most urgent reform: Deregulation. Zero fiscal cost. Give certainty to business. "Don't teach business. Don't disturb business."
▸ Most promising sector (10 years): Semiconductors & nickel batteries. AI and digitalization require portable power. Indonesia controls nickel. But it must join the global supply chain. If Indonesia merely bans exports without integrating, the world will innovate around nickel entirely.
▸ Talent or natural resources: TALENT. Without question.
▸ Growth or equity: GROWTH. (With hesitation, but growth must come first.)
▸ Quality most needing strengthening: Human Capital.

Rapid Fire — Part II
▸ One word for Indonesia's economy in 2030: "DISAPPOINT". It disappoints optimists (expectations too high) and pessimists (it doesn't collapse either). Hopefully this prediction is wrong.
▸ Indonesia in 2045: Hopefully "pass the group stage" — meaning, graduate from middle-income status. Even this is doubtful if current trajectory continues. The target is high. The real risk is the middle-income trap, not crisis.
▸ Star to shine in World Cup 2026: Lionel Messi. His sixth World Cup. Probably too old. But the heart wants what it wants.
▸ World Cup 2026 champion: Hope: Argentina. Rational guess: Spain.
