Mastering Fire

Fire is the foundation. Learn to build, maintain, and read the flames that create legendary barbecue.

Building

Building the Foundation

Start with a dense log in the middle, surrounded by dry small pieces. Space wood with 1-inch gaps for airflow. The "log cabin" method gets your cooker to 275-300°F in 30 minutes.

30 min to temp →
Wood

Post Oak & Wood Selection

Central Texas barbecue is defined by post oak. Aged 1-2 years is the sweet spot. Pick up a piece — feel its density and moisture. Heavy = more moisture = slower burn.

1-2 years aged →
Management

Working the Fire

Be proactive, not reactive. Time your logs so as one fire dies, another catches. Keep temperatures within 5°F. Listen for pops and cracks — they tell you how fast wood is burning.

±5°F precision →
Smoke

Reading the Smoke

Blue, clean smoke is the goal. Brown or black smoke means dirty fire. At 600-680°F combustion, smoke becomes water vapor. Bad smoke for 10-30 minutes will not ruin an 8-hour cook.

Visual cues →
Building

Offset Cooker Mechanics

Heat hits a flap, curls like a tornado, creating convection. No dampers needed — just control airflow with the firebox door. Open = more air = hotter fire.

Airflow science →
Management

Pro Fire Tips

Use greasy butcher paper to start fires. Pre-warm air by keeping coals at the back of the firebox. Lay logs flat to slow burn, stand them up to increase heat. Always watch the smokestack.

The Meats

From pork butt to brisket — the cuts, the rubs, and the techniques that define Texas barbecue.

Overview

Pork Butt — The Forgiving Cut

From the shoulder, bone-in preferred. Cook until the bone twists out easily. Equal parts salt and pepper, plus paprika for color. Mustard slather helps bark stick.

Overview

Spare Ribs — The Intermediate Cut

Full spares, not St. Louis cut. Trim sternum and skirt. 2:1 pepper to salt ratio. Leave membrane on — it protects from overcooking and adds texture.

~6 hours →
Technique

Franklin's Rib Method

3 hours unwrapped at 270°F. Sauce and wrap for 2 hours. Unwrap for final 1 hour. Feel for tenderness at the third rib cartilage — that is your test spot.

3-2-1 method →
Sauce

Rib Sauce Formula

Dilute your favorite sauce 1:1 with apple cider vinegar. Warm it before applying. Less sugar = less burning. Apply before wrapping so it cooks into the meat.

Acid-balanced →
Selection

Choosing the Perfect Brisket

Prime grade, Angus preferred. Look for thick flat, good marbling. Avoid frozen — it gets mushy. Floppy = good fat-to-meat ratio. The thicker, the better.

Prime + Angus →
Trimming

The Trim — Make It Aerodynamic

Trim to 1/4 inch fat cap. Remove hard seam fat. Round all edges — no sharp corners. Cut off the deckle (where carcass was split). Think "little Maserati."

Curved boning knife →
Seasoning

Salt & Pepper — Nothing Else

50/50 kosher salt and 16-mesh black pepper by volume. Mustard slather as glue. Season from high for even coverage. Let sit 30-45 min before cooking.

Simplicity wins →
Cooking

The 12-Hour Cook

Start at 255°F. Spritz at 3 hours. Bump to 265°F at 5 hours. Wrap at 8-9 hours (end of stall) in butcher paper. Finish at 275-285°F. Rest until 140-150°F internal.

Low and slow →
Science

Understanding The Stall

At 160-170°F internal, muscle tightens and sweats — evaporative cooling. Do not panic! Bump temps to 280°F. Wrap at the tail end of stall when bark is set.

~165°F internal →
Slicing

The Money Shot

Split point from flat. Slice flat against grain, pencil-thick. Slice point into burnt ends. Every bite should have bark, fat, meat, salt, and pepper. Serve immediately.

Against the grain →
Steak

Hot & Fast Steak

Create hot and cool zones. Flip frequently — do not believe "flip once" myth. Move to cool zone to control cook. Feel for doneness, do not trust thumb test.

Zone cooking →
Vegetables

Charred Vegetables

Use a charcoal chimney for extreme heat. Broccolini: stems over flame, crowns off to side. Oil, salt, quick char. Think texture — crisp inside, charred outside.

Equipment

What Makes a Good Cooker

  • Thick metal (1/4 inch) Retains heat, lasts forever
  • Long firebox Room to build and adjust fire
  • Single grate, no shelves No dripping between meats
  • Thermometer at grate level Not on the lid — that is wrong
  • Smokestack placement Pulls heat across the grates
