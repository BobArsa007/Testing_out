Three Faces of Scarcity

These stories illustrate a central hypothesis: because the focus on scarcity is involuntary, it impedes our ability to focus on anything else. Scarcity in one walk of life means we have less attention, less mind, in the rest of life.

💼
The Executive

The Pitch and the Game

A last-minute sales pitch collides with a daughter's championship softball game. Even while cheering, the mind wanders back to work.

One of your biggest clients has informed you that it will be taking its business elsewhere. You convince the account manager to listen to one last pitch tomorrow. You cancel all meetings and put off all other tasks. One appointment cannot be avoided: your daughter's city championship softball game tonight.

"On the way to the game, your daughter realizes she forgot her lucky charm. You snap at her before turning around to pick it up."

At the game, you cannot enjoy yourself. Your mind keeps turning to that presentation. Not that you can work on it now — you just cannot focus on the game. When your daughter occasionally catches a glimpse of you, she knows it. The jubilation of her team's win helps cover your mistakes, but your performance that evening would not put you in any parenting Hall of Fame.

The Student

Tuition and the Exam

John patched together loans and scholarships to attend a prestigious college. Then two scholarships evaporated. With the exam looming, his mind is elsewhere.

John is putting himself through college. Though his parents saved for their kids' education, they did not save enough. By the time his turn came, the fund was meager and tuition was even higher. He patched together student loans, financial aid, and scholarships. It was messy, but somehow he made it work.

Until now. Two scholarships that were to be automatically renewed have suddenly evaporated; the foundations were hit hard by the recession. How would he make tuition for next semester? The payment was due in less than a month. Could he afford another loan? Should he transfer to the local college?

"John just can't focus. He keeps thinking about what to do. Preoccupied, he misses a study group that he needed to attend."

When the exam day arrives, he tries to focus, but his mind keeps going elsewhere. He misses some easy questions and is doubly upset: not only is he struggling with tuition; he is annoyed at his abysmal performance on the exam.

The Manager

The Unreliable Employee

A fast-food manager sees low-wage workers as careless and snippy. But perhaps their minds are simply elsewhere — on the scarcity of making ends meet.

A manager of a fast-food burger shop laments his trouble with his low-wage employees. "They are just so unreliable," he says. Most of his time is spent cajoling them into behaving better with customers. "Put on a smile. Be friendly. When the customer is a jerk, don't get snippy."

The rest of his time is spent dealing with careless mistakes. "When someone says they want medium fries, how hard is it to press the button that says 'fries'?" He is clearly frustrated. "Maybe it's that they just don't care. Maybe it's the education in this country. Maybe it's the way they were raised."

"We catch the low-income worker not when she is at home managing her finances but when she is at work serving food."

The manager attributes the behavior to personality or upbringing. But these employees are not unskilled or uncaring. Their bandwidth is simply taxed by the persistent monetary concerns that scarcity imposes — concerns every bit as loud as a passing train.

Bandwidth Tax: Professional Composure

What is Bandwidth?

Psychologists use several fine distinctions to capture the idea of "less mind." We use the umbrella term bandwidth to cover them all. It measures our computational capacity — our ability to pay attention, make good decisions, stick with plans, and resist temptations.

Bandwidth correlates with everything from intelligence and SAT performance to impulse control and success on diets. This chapter makes a bold claim: by constantly drawing us back into the tunnel, scarcity taxes our bandwidth and inhibits our most fundamental capacities.

We can think of bandwidth as comprising two broad components:

Cognitive Capacity Fluid Intelligence

The mechanisms underlying our ability to solve problems, retain information, and engage in logical reasoning.

Executive Control Self-Regulation

Our ability to manage cognitive activities: planning, attention, initiating and inhibiting actions, and controlling impulses.

It's Loud In Here

The things that distract us need not come from outside. We often generate them for ourselves, and these internal distractions can disrupt our attention more than a physical train.

Imagine sitting in an office near the railroad tracks. Trains rattle by several times an hour. They are not deafening, but they pull you away from what you were doing. The interruption is brief, but its effect lasts longer. You need time to refocus.

This mirrors the conditions of a school in New Haven located next to a noisy railroad line. Researchers found that sixth graders on the train side were a full year behind their counterparts on the quieter side. When the city installed noise pads, the difference disappeared.

Now picture yourself in a quiet office, but you are struggling with your mortgage. Your mind wanders: Should we sell the second car? Should we take another loan? These noisy trains of thought are every bit as hard to ignore. They arrive with even greater regularity and are every bit as uninvited.

"These trains of thought rumble with personal relevance. The mortgage distraction lingers because it matters. It is a distraction precisely because it causes us to tunnel."

Executive Control

If scarcity reduces bandwidth, it should also reduce self-control. Executive control underlies our ability to manage cognitive activities, resist impulses, and override automatic responses.

The Marshmallow Test & Beyond

In Walter Mischel's famous study, children who resisted the marshmallow did so by focusing their attention elsewhere. "Once you realize that willpower is just a matter of learning how to control your attention and thoughts," Mischel noted, "you can really begin to increase it."

In a related experiment, subjects rehearsing a seven-digit number chose cake over fruit 50% more often than those rehearsing a two-digit number. When our mental bandwidth is occupied, we have less capacity to prevent automatic choices.

In another study, subjects served a revolting chicken foot (claws intact) by a Chinese experimenter. Those with loaded minds blurted out rude comments despite their best intentions. A tax on bandwidth makes it harder to control our impulses.

Other Forms of Scarcity

The bandwidth tax is not limited to money. It appears wherever scarcity captures the mind — in dieting, in loneliness, and even in artificial games with limited resources.

Dieting
Loneliness
Artificial Scarcity

"While a tenth of my mind was devoted to school, and a tenth to my daughter, the other 70 percent was constantly focused on food — the calorie count of a grape, the filling bulk of popcorn."

— Natalie Kusz, "The Fat Lady Sings"

Dieters, when doing anything, should find they have fewer mental resources because they are partly preoccupied with food. Studies comparing dieters to nondieters find that people simply perform worse on a variety of cognitive tests when they are dieting.

These effects appear even when there is no weight loss, and physiological measures show that nutritional deficiencies are not the cause. While losing weight, you face a bandwidth tax. If you settle into a new equilibrium and no longer need to restrain eating, the tax disappears.

The Dichotic Listening Task

Subjects listen to two different sounds, one in each ear. Most people are right-ear dominant for language. When asked to track the right ear, the lonely and nonlonely do equally well.

But focusing on the nondominant left ear requires bandwidth. It requires executive control to override the natural urge. Here, the lonely did significantly worse. They were less effective at tuning out the right ear and listening to the left.

In another study, researchers randomly assigned subjects feedback suggesting they would be either socially well-adjusted or very lonely. After the information sank in, those who anticipated loneliness did much worse on Raven's tests.

Brain scans showed that anticipating loneliness reduced activation of executive control areas. When given chocolate-chip cookies, they ate roughly twice as many. Consistent with this, older adults who feel lonely consume substantially more fatty foods.

The Angry Blueberries

In laboratory games where subjects were given fewer resources ("poor" players with fewer blueberries), the same pattern emerged. Even though the poor players played far shorter games, they were so focused on managing their limited resources that they had less bandwidth afterward.

After the game, these "poor" subjects performed worse on the heart-flower executive control task. Like the dieters, the money-poor, and the lonely, the blueberry-poor subjects were taxed by scarcity. The effect is not about the specific domain — it is about the mindset of scarcity itself.

What the Bandwidth Tax Means

The bandwidth tax changes us in surprising and powerful ways. So much of what we attribute to talent or personality is actually predicated on cognitive capacity and executive control.

"The problem is not the person but the context of scarcity."

Imagine you are sitting at a computer unaware of background programs. As your browser crawls, you might think, What a slow computer. You confuse the processor loaded down by other tasks for one that is inherently slow. Similarly, it is easy to confuse a mind loaded by scarcity for one that is inherently less capable.

The restaurant manager attributes his employees' behavior to lack of skill or motivation. The harried sales manager, when she snaps at her daughter, looks like a bad parent. The financially strapped student who misses easy questions looks incapable or lazy. But these people are not unskilled or uncaring. They are heavily taxed.

We must broaden our notion of scarcity. When we think of having very little, we focus on the physical implications: less time for fun, less money to spend. The bandwidth tax suggests there is another, perhaps more important, shortfall. Scarcity doesn't just lead us to overborrow or fail to invest. It leaves us handicapped. It makes us dumber. It makes us more impulsive. We must get by with less mind available — making life that much harder.
