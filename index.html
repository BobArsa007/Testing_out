The Psychology of Scarcity

Packing and Slack

How scarcity changes the way we think, choose, and live — and why having less forces us to think harder about every decision we make.

01 / The Metaphor

The Suitcase Problem

Imagine packing for a business trip. The size of your suitcase changes everything — not just what you bring, but how you think about what you bring.

Both suitcases impose limits, but only the small suitcase really feels like a problem.

You are about to leave on a business trip. Imagine how you might pack a suitably large suitcase. You might start by putting in all the essentials — toiletries, business clothes, electronics. With room left over, you might add a few less essential items. You pack an umbrella in case it rains. You take a sweater in case it is cold. You pack your gym clothes and running shoes. Satisfied, you close the suitcase with some room to spare.

Now imagine instead packing a small suitcase for the same trip. As before, you might start by casually tossing in the bare essentials. But these already quickly fill the suitcase. You take everything out and pack again, this time more methodically. You carefully stack and arrange. You become creative in making room. You stuff socks and a phone charger inside your shoes and uncoil your belt and slide it along the suitcase edge.

⏱️

Time Suitcase

We have a time suitcase that must fit our work, leisure, and family time. When time is scarce, every hour requires careful accounting.

💰

Money Suitcase

We have a money suitcase into which we must fit our housing, clothing, and all our expenses. Small budgets make every purchase consequential.

🍽️

Calorie Suitcase

Some of us even have a self-imposed calorie suitcase into which we must fit all our meals. Diets make us calorie accountants.

When scarcity focuses us, it also changes how we pack. It changes how we manage each dollar, each hour, or each calorie. It also leaves us with differently packed suitcases. The big suitcase is packed carelessly, with room to spare. The small suitcase is packed carefully and tightly.

02 / Decision Making

Trade-off Thinking

Scarcity forces us to recognize that putting in one item means some other item must come out. But when resources feel abundant, trade-offs disappear from our minds.

The cost of one modern heavy bomber is this: a modern brick school in more than thirty cities. It is two electric power plants each serving a town of 60,000 people. It is two fine, fully equipped hospitals. It is some fifty miles of concrete highway.

— Dwight Eisenhower, 1953

You are at a restaurant, having dinner with friends. The waiter describes the specials and then asks if you'd like to have a drink. You don't typically order a cocktail but something on the menu catches your eye. How do you decide whether to order it?

You may calculate how long before you'll need to drive. You may wait to see if any of your friends order drinks. You may even wonder whether you'll be splitting the bill. Or you may consider whether $10 is a reasonable price. What's notable, though, is the dog that didn't bark. There is one question you don't ask yourself: "If I buy this drink, what will I not buy instead?"

03 / The Concept

Understanding Slack

Slack is the space left untapped because of the way we pack. It is typical of large suitcases — and abundant budgets. It is the by-product of packing under abundance.

The packing metaphor illustrates why scarcity creates trade-off thinking. We pack big suitcases loosely. Not every nook and cranny is filled. There is space left unused here and there. We call this space slack — the part of our budget that is left untapped because of the way we pack.

Imagine that after having packed a large suitcase, you want to add an item. You can just throw it in. No item needs to come out. You do not need to rearrange the contents because the suitcase had extra room to begin with — it had slack. But with a smaller suitcase, adding something necessitates taking something out.

🕐

Slack in Time

On a not-too-busy week, we leave holes in our schedule. You leave a fifteen-minute window between meetings, where in busier times you would have squeezed in a quick phone call.

💳

Slack in Money

High-income shoppers are twice as likely to report that they do not track their spending because they "don't have to; [they] make enough money."

🎯

Intended vs. Natural

Intended slack is carefully budgeted room for the unexpected. Natural slack is the by-product of packing under abundance — space left unused without deliberate planning.

During good times, we don't meticulously account for every dollar. We generally choose a house and a car that leave us a comfortable amount of room for everything else. We have a rough sense of what kind of restaurants we ought to patronize and how often, so we can stay broadly within our budget. This mindset is a feature of abundance, and slack is the result.

04 / Nature's Lesson

Poor Bees and Rich Wasps

Why do bees create such precise structures and wasps such messy ones? The answer reveals a deep truth about how scarcity shapes behavior.

🐝 Scarce Resource: Wax

The Honeybee

Each pound of wax requires eight pounds of honey, which requires more than ninety thousand individual bee trips. The bees create walls that meet at a remarkably precise 120 degrees, forming hexagons perfect to the eye.

Builds with precision because waste is costly.

🐝 Abundant Resource: Mud

The Mud Dauber Wasp

The wasps build their nests out of mud. The cells are roughly cylindrical, but they are plastered together erratically, with none of the precision of the hive.

Builds sloppily because material is cheap.

Something similar happens for the poor and the rich. Imagine that before packing a suitcase, you lay on the bed the items you want to take with you, with the most valuable items on the left and the least valuable ones on the right. For a three-day trip, the first pair of underwear would be on the far left; a fifth pair would be on the far right.

You start putting the items in your suitcase starting with the most valuable, from left to right. You can pack quite a few items before your suitcase is full, by which point you'd be packing things you do not care much about, like the fifth pair of underwear. Unused room in the suitcases of the rich comes at the expense of items of little consequence. The suitcases of the poor get full while they are still packing items they very much need.

There is an almost economic logic to all this: the poor have less slack because they can afford less of it. Packing material — space in the suitcase — is cheap for the rich, like mud, but it is expensive for the poor, like wax. So the rich pack like wasps, casually, inefficiently, and with slack. And the poor pack like bees, carefully, and with no slack.

05 / Consequences

What We Buy With Slack

Slack frees us from trade-offs. It licenses us to buy items that on their own, devoid of any other considerations, have some appeal. The result, of course, is inefficiency and waste.

1

Cabinet Castaways

Kitchen cabinets across the United States are full of soups, jams, and canned food that have not been used for ages. Some estimates suggest that one in ten items bought in the grocery store is destined to become a cabinet castaway.

2

The Storage Economy

Over $12 billion is spent annually on self-storage, three times as much as is spent on music purchases. The United States has more than two billion square feet allocated to self-storage space.

3

Time Evaporation

When we have plenty of time, we loll around, and time evaporates. Minutes here and there add up to hours frittered away. We end up getting six hours of good use out of a sixteen-hour day.

4

The Paradox of Choice

When given two appealing alternatives instead of one, more people chose the original plan. Slack provides an easy way to avoid the burden of choosing — it allows you to say, "I'll take both."

A house is just a pile of stuff with a cover on it.

— George Carlin

06 / Hidden Value

Slack not only absolves you of the need to make trade-offs. It means mistakes do not entail real sacrifice. It gives us room to maneuver, to reshuffle when we err.

The planning fallacy is common to many people, but not everybody experiences the same consequences from it. Suppose you have a project due at the end of the month. In reality the project requires forty hours of work, but you mistakenly think it will take only thirty, and you plan accordingly. As the deadline approaches, your error becomes clear. You are ten hours short.

Suppose you are not terribly busy. The shortfall is nothing more than an annoyance. You look at your schedule and find ways to create time. There are a few commitments that can be easily moved around, a few to-dos that can be put off and, most important, you have empty spaces here and there in your calendar already. With a little jiggling, you're set.

Suppose, instead, that you are already heavily committed this week. Now this is more than annoying. Like a wobbly Jenga tower, if you delay or move any one thing around, the whole structure will collapse. For the less busy person, slack absorbs the error, thus minimizing the consequences. The busy person, on the other hand, cannot shrug it off so easily. Each added hour must come at the expense of something else.

Scarcity not only raises the costs of error; it also provides more opportunity to err, to make misguided choices. It is harder to do things right, because many items — time commitments for the busy, expenses for the poor — must be carefully made to fit into a constrained budget.

07 / Synthesis

Scarcity and Slack

Physical limits and trade-offs are always there. But we do not experience them that way. The concept of slack cuts to the core of the psychology of scarcity.

🧮

Mathematical Reality

Scarcity may create a logistically harder packing problem. The mind, challenged by the psychology that emerges from scarcity, may find itself needing to navigate a world that is computationally more complex.

🧠

Psychological Reality

While actual limits and trade-offs are universal, the experience is not. A big suitcase does not just permit more room; it removes the feeling of scarcity entirely.

✨

The Luxury of Slack

Slack is not just inefficiency; it is a mental luxury. Abundance affords us the luxury of packing poorly, the luxury of not having to think, as well as the luxury of not minding mistakes.

A man is rich in proportion to the number of things he can afford to let alone.

— Henry David Thoreau

Having slack allows us the feeling of abundance. Slack is not just inefficiency; it is a mental luxury. Abundance does not just allow us to buy more goods. It affords us the luxury of packing poorly, the luxury of not having to think, as well as the luxury of not minding mistakes.
