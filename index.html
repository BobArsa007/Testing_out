🌫️ Smoke Color Guide

Click each type to learn what it means and how to fix it.

White / Steamy Smoke

Fresh wood starting to catch — water vapor evaporating

Thin Blue Smoke

Perfect clean combustion — the holy grail of barbecue

Brown / Billowy Smoke

Incomplete combustion — creosote forming, bitter flavors

Black Smoke

Starved of oxygen — choked fire, acrid and ruined meat

⏱️ Fire Tending Timeline
0:00 — Start
Build the Foundation
Log cabin style with 1" gaps for airflow
Use one dense log in the middle surrounded by smaller dry splits. Space wood about 1 inch apart. Place oily butcher paper 3 inches from the back. Light and watch. Within 30 minutes you should reach 275-300°F. This is a great time to season your meat.
0:30 — First Hour
Watch the Smoke Stack
Learn to read airflow and combustion stage
Fresh logs always smoke initially. Watch for flames intertwining between logs. You should see smoke actually exiting the smokestack. Give it a blow if needed. The fire should pull air through naturally — you cannot push air on a fire, but you can pull it.
Every 10-15 min
Add One Log at a Time
Proactive fire management beats reactive
Time your log additions so the new log catches right as the previous one starts declining. Listen for popping and cracking — that tells you the wood is burning rapidly. A heavy log will steam moisture out audibly. If temps drop too low, use a dry light piece. If too high, lay a log flat to restrict airflow underneath.
As Needed
Rake Coals & Reset
Break down ash, rebuild coal bed
When a big fire burns down, get in with a shovel. Rake out coals, break down the coal bed. Blackened logs that have burned complete can be buried in coals. Logs that haven't burned complete should be turned sideways and given air channels underneath. Pre-warm incoming air by keeping coals toward the back of the firebox in cold weather.
✅ Fire Management Checklist
  • Use seasoned post oak, aged 1-2 years
  • Build log cabin with 1" air gaps
  • Start with oily butcher paper or grape seed oil
  • Keep firebox door open in good weather
  • Never choke fire by shutting door completely
  • Add logs proactively, not reactively
  • Aim for thin blue smoke (clean combustion)
  • Maintain cooker within 5° of target
  • Listen for popping = rapid burn; steaming = moisture
  • Rake coals and reset when fire burns down

📊 The 12-Hour Timeline

Trimming & Seasoning

  • Keep brisket cold — warm fat is sticky and hard to trim
  • Remove the deckle (where carcass was split) — it won't render
  • Trim sides to aerodynamic curves — no sharp edges that will dry out
  • Leave ¼ inch of soft, pillowy fat (not hard waxy fat)
  • Remove thick seam fat between flat and point (won't render)
  • Season: 50/50 kosher salt + 16 mesh black pepper by volume
  • Light mustard slather as glue — you won't taste it after 12 hours
  • Season presentation side (fat side up) most carefully
  • Let temper 30-45 minutes before putting on cooker

Hours 0-3: Foundation

  • Start cooker at 255°F — ease into it, don't shock the meat
  • Place point toward fire (more fat = more protection)
  • Use water pan to keep moisture in cook chamber
  • First 3 hours: do NOT open lid — let bark begin to form
  • Watch smokestack for clean, thin smoke
  • At 3-hour mark: first check — look for edge drying, bark formation
  • Spritz edges with apple cider vinegar if looking dry

Hours 3-8: Building Bark

  • Check every hour — spritz dry spots specifically
  • Bump temps to 265°F around hour 5 if bark is forming well
  • Look for mahogany color, fat beginning to render
  • Brisket will shrink significantly — this is normal
  • Edges should not be black or crispy — if so, temps too high
  • Internal temp will climb steadily toward 160°F
  • Start planning wrap materials (butcher paper) around hour 7

The Stall (160-170°F internal)

  • Muscle tightens and squeezes out moisture (evaporative cooling)
  • Surface gets very wet — this is normal and temporary
  • Bump cooker to 280°F+ to push through — moisture protects from burning
  • Can last 1-3 hours — don't panic, it's part of the process
  • Once surface dries and bark looks crusty, you're through the stall
  • Wrap RIGHT at the end of the stall — not before, not long after

Wrap & Finish (Hours 8-12)

  • Use pink butcher paper (not foil — too "pot roasty")
  • Double-layer the bottom where juices will collect
  • Wrap tight — conform to brisket shape, no loose bag
  • Put back on cooker, point toward fire
  • Cook at 275-285°F wrapped
  • Internal target: 200-205°F, but FEEL trumps temperature
  • Pick up with towel — should feel like "a bag of melted butter"
  • Flat should fall over your fingers when lifted

Rest & Slice

  • Rest wrapped until internal temp drops to 140-150°F (about 45 min)
  • DO NOT put in cooler while hot — carries over too far
  • Unwrap — save all juices on cutting board
  • Separate flat from point along the seam
  • Slice flat against grain, pencil-thick (¼ inch)
  • Slice point perpendicular, slightly thicker
  • Every slice should have bark, fat, meat, salt, pepper
  • Serve immediately — brisket oxidizes quickly once cut

🔪 Trimming Spare Ribs
Step 1
Remove the Sternum
Cut off the cartilage breast bone for even cooking
The sternum is the bone at the wide end. Cut it off completely — it gets in the way when feeling for tenderness later. Use a sharp chef's knife and cut through the cartilage. This also helps the rack cook more evenly.
Step 2
Square the Rack
Create uniform thickness for even airflow
Trim the edges to create a uniform rectangular shape. Round off corners so air flows over smoothly (Franklin calls this making it look like a "little Maserati"). Remove the skirt (flap of meat on the bone side) — it cooks unevenly and slides off.
Step 3
The Membrane Decision
Franklin leaves it on — here's why
Most people remove the membrane. Franklin leaves it on because: (1) at the restaurant it would take hours to remove from hundreds of racks, (2) it allows slight overcooking while maintaining structural integrity, (3) rub penetrates through it anyway, and (4) it protects the meat from drying. Make your own choice.
Step 4
Check for Bones
Feel for chipped bone fragments
Run your fingers along the bones. If the processor sawed too fast, bone chips can splinter off. You don't want anyone biting into a bone shard. Remove any sharp fragments. Also check for "shiners" — ribs where the meat has been cut so thin you can see the bone through it. Avoid these at the store.
🧂 Seasoning Technique
  • 2 parts pepper to 1 part salt (opposite of brisket — ribs are thinner)
  • Add paprika for color — gives a nice mahogany red
  • Light mustard slather — just enough to make tacky, not wet
  • Season parallel to the bones (typewriter motion)
  • Hold shaker 8-10 inches high for even distribution
  • Pat down gently — don't rub it in aggressively
  • Add extra pepper on fatty patches (smoke sticks better to pepper than fat)
  • Put extra salt on membrane side for tongue-first salt hit
✅ Ribs Checklist
  • Buy full spare ribs with good marbling (avoid "enhanced" with salt water)
  • Remove sternum and square the rack
  • Remove skirt meat from bone side
  • Check for bone chips and shiners
  • Light mustard slather
  • 2:1 pepper to salt, plus paprika
  • Cook 3 hours unwrapped at 270°F
  • Spritz every hour after first hour
  • Sauce both sides, let set 10 min
  • Wrap tight in foil, double-layer over bones
  • Cook 2 more hours wrapped
  • Feel for tenderness — bend test over fingers
  • Rest 30-40 minutes before cutting
  • Pour saved juices over ribs before serving

🐷 Pork Butt

The forgiving beginner's cook — hard to mess up

📋 Pork Butt Planner
🧂 Heavy Seasoning Method
"For pork butt, since it's all going to get pulled apart anyway, I feel pretty good about using a lot of mustard or hot sauce, putting a good slather on there. The more seasoning on the outside, the better it mixes in later." — Aaron Franklin

⏱️ The Cook Timeline
  • Buy bone-in pork butt (shoulder blade bone) — bone slides out when done
  • Minimal trimming — leave fat cap, remove only excessive hard fat
  • Heavy mustard slather — much more than brisket or ribs
  • Equal parts salt and pepper, plus paprika for color
  • Season VERY heavily — interior meat is bland, needs exterior flavor to mix in
  • Place fat side up (heat comes from top on Franklin's cookers)
  • Cook at 270°F — forgiving temperature
  • Start spritzing at hour 3 with apple cider vinegar
  • Spritz every hour until wrap
  • Use sweeping motion — don't mist (evaporates) or blast (knocks off bark)
  • Watch for fat cap to split — this is your visual wrap cue
  • Edges should not be crispy or black
  • Internal temp is irrelevant for wrap decision — go by bark and fat split
  • Wrap tight in foil, shiny side in (dull side out absorbs heat)
  • Double-layer the foil for strength
  • Cook 1.5-2 more hours until internal temp ~203°F
  • FEEL is more important than temp — should feel like "bag of melted butter"
  • Rest 45-60 minutes wrapped
  • Pull apart with hands (or tongs if too hot)
  • Mix bark flakes back into meat
  • Save juices from foil — pour over pulled pork
✅ Pork Butt Checklist
  • Buy bone-in pork butt (shoulder blade bone)
  • Minimal trimming — leave fat cap on
  • Heavy mustard slather all over
  • Equal parts salt & pepper, plus paprika
  • Cook at 270°F for ~8 hours unwrapped
  • Spritz with cider vinegar every hour from hour 3
  • Wrap when fat cap splits (visual cue)
  • Finish 1.5-2 hours wrapped until 203°F
  • Rest 45-60 minutes before pulling
  • Bone should slide out cleanly when done

⚡ Hot & Fast Grilling

Direct fire cooking for steaks, vegetables, and quick meals

🔥 Two-Zone Setup
🔴

Hot Zone

Directly over coals/wood. For searing, charring, and developing crust. Use for initial contact and final finishing.

🟡

Safety Zone

Indirect heat, off to the side. For tempering cold meat, slowing down cooks, and managing flare-ups.

🪵

Wood Chunk

Place a dense oak log on the hot zone. It provides smoke flavor and creates a natural divider between zones.

🌡️

Charcoal Chimney

Start with natural briquettes. Let them ash over completely. Dump and create your zones immediately.

🥩 Steak Technique
Prep
Temper & Season
15-20 min out of fridge, grape seed oil, light salt
Small steaks don't need long tempering. Use grape seed oil (high smoke point, inexpensive). Light salt now — you'll salt again after cooking. Don't overseason before the fire.
Sear
High Heat Contact
Place on hot zone, flip frequently
The old "flip once" rule is silly. Flip constantly to even out the cook. Move to safety zone if cooking too fast. Look for meat fibers tightening and surface drying. Use oil to soften dry spots.
Feel
Trust Your Fingers
No thermometer — squeeze and learn
The thumb-palm test is wrong — everyone's hands are different. Every steak is different. The ONLY way to learn is to pull, cut, check, and remember. Over time you develop muscle memory for medium-rare vs medium.
Rest
Let It Settle
5-10 minutes, reabsorbs moisture
If you cut immediately, steam escapes = moisture loss. Let it cool slightly. The fibers relax and reabsorb juices. Slice against the grain for tenderness. Finish with flaky salt.
🥦 Broccolini / Vegetables
  • Use a cooling rack turned upside-down over the chimney
  • Keep stalks over direct flame, crowns on the cooler edge
  • Oil, salt, then char quickly — keep moving constantly
  • Stalks need more heat and time; crowns need quick blistering
  • Remove when stalks are slightly soft with charred blisters
  • This two-zone thinking directly applies to brisket (flat vs point)

Surprisingly heavy, solid center. Great for early cook when you want clean flavors.

Quick catch, fast burn. Use for small cookers or quick temp boosts.

⚠️ Wood Quality Guide

Green Wood (Fresh Cut)

Too much moisture — won't reach clean combustion, smolders, bitter smoke

Seasoned (1-2 Years)

The sweet spot — moisture mostly gone, structure intact, predictable burn

Overly Dry / Rotten

Decomposed too much — nothing left to burn, no flavor, turns to dust

🔧 Equipment & Setup

Understanding your cooker and essential tools

🔥 Offset Cooker Anatomy
📦

Firebox

Should be long enough to build a fire "back" from the cook chamber. Round fireboxes are ideal for log placement. No dampers — control with door position.

🌪️

Cook Chamber

Single grate preferred (no dripping between shelves). Heat hits a baffle plate, curls like a tornado, creating chaotic convection that dries the bark.

🌫️

Smoke Stack

Massive airflow = massive vacuum. No damper on stack. Watch the smoke to guide your fire — it's your most important instrument.

🌡️

Thermometer

One gauge at the hottest spot (near the firebox end) is enough. It's a loose guide — watch the smoke, watch the meat, feel the fire.

🔧 Cheap Cooker Mods
  • Baffle plate: Add a piece of metal (old license plate works) to deflect heat from going straight up the stack
  • Thermometer relocation: Drill hole, add threaded fitting at grate level — not on the lid
  • Seal leaks: High-temp gasket around doors to control airflow
  • Extend smokestack: Taller stack = stronger draft = better airflow
  • Tuning plates: Add adjustable plates under grate to even out hot spots
  • Charcoal basket: For smaller cookers, contains coals and improves airflow

📚 History & Philosophy

Where Texas barbecue comes from and why it matters

🕰️ Central Texas Barbecue Timeline
Early 1900s
German & Czech Markets
Immigrants bring butcher shops and meat markets to Central Texas
German and Czech immigrants settled in Central Texas and opened small grocery stores and meat markets. They butchered cows in the back. Before refrigeration, unsold meat had to be cooked or it would spoil. They dug brick pits in the ground — fires on one end, grates on the other, stacks to draw smoke. This is the origin of "pit" barbecue.
Post-WWII
The Boom Years
Returning veterans open joints; 1948 is a magic year
Military men returned with a little money and needed work. Many opened barbecue joints in the late 1940s. Look at any historic Texas barbecue joint — odds are it opened in 1948. They started as markets with smokehouses in back. The meat was whatever didn't sell that week: hind quarters, fore quarters, tenderloins, not specifically brisket yet.
1950s
Boxed Beef Revolution
Refrigerated trucks and processing plants change everything
Before the 1950s, markets bought whole animals and broke them down. With boxed beef and refrigerated transport, they could order specific cuts. Brisket was cheap and tough — perfect for slow smoking. By the 1960s, brisket had become THE Texas barbecue cut. It was never prestigious; it was what you cooked because you had to.
Traditional Meal
Meat, Bread, Pickles, Onion
The German market heritage on a plate
The classic sides — white bread, pickles, sliced onion, maybe tomato or cheese — all came from the grocery store side of the business. You'd go in the back, buy smoked meat, then go up front and get a loaf of bread, a jar of pickles, an onion. Eat it in the parking lot with your pocket knife. No sauce needed if the meat was good.
Today
Franklin Barbecue & The New School
Precision, passion, and the line around the block
Aaron Franklin started with a $99 offset smoker in his backyard in 2009. Through obsessive fire management, precise trimming, and relentless attention to detail, Franklin Barbecue became a destination. The line is part of the experience. Aaron still believes you learn by making bad barbecue first — and that the fire, not the meat, is what matters most.
