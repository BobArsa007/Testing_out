The Starting Point

Two Stories, One Psychology

How a professor overwhelmed with projects and a man deep in debt revealed the universal mechanism of scarcity.

Sendhil Mullainathan was overwhelmed. Too many projects, everything running late, constantly behind. He called Eldar Shafir with a plan: say no to everything new, pay off old projects, then be judicious going forward. A week later, he called back: "There's this great new project we should take on."

Meanwhile, Shawn was deep in debt. He too had a plan: pay down debts, then spend judiciously. The next week, he bought a jacket he couldn't afford.

Two different people. One experiencing scarcity of time, the other of money. Yet they were doing the exact same thing. This insight transformed into a deeper understanding: poverty is a special case of scarcity. The busy are scarce in time. The lonely are scarce in friends. Even dieters are scarce in calories.

The Central Metaphor

The Suitcase

Think of your budget as a suitcase. Some have large suitcases with slack. Others have tiny, tightly packed ones. Life feels very different.

The Large Suitcase (Slack)
You toss in what you need. You might add lower-utility items. You leave slack for unexpected events. When you see shoes you like, you ask: "Is the price right?" If yes, you buy them. Simple.

The Psychology of Not Having Enough

Scarcity creates a specific psychology that operates across domains — money, time, food, and social connection.

⚖️

Trade-off Thinking

When you buy a TV or toaster, do you think about what you might not be able to buy instead? Low-income individuals report thinking about trade-offs far more often. Every purchase above a muffin requires calculating what is sacrificed.

🎯

Price Expertise

The rich don't know what a cab meter starts at. The poor are three times more likely to know. Not because they ride more cabs, but because they must solve these problems constantly. Scarcity breeds expertise in costs.

🍕

Temptations

Things that ought to be regular — taking cabs, buying pizzas, spending an hour with your kids — become temptations that must be resisted. Resisting temptation is depleting. The poor resist far more often, and it fatigues them.

🎁

Indulgences

Critiqued for buying small luxuries while in debt, the poor are actually behaving exactly like the busy academic who misses a deadline yet sits schmoozing at a talk. The psychology of "I cannot pay the debt, but now I can do something nice" is universal.

🔍

Tunneling

Scarcity focuses you automatically on what you lack. Thirsty people identify water-related words faster at 300 milliseconds — pre-consciously. You cannot control this. It leaves less room for everything else.

🧠

Bandwidth Tax

The constant mental load of scarcity directly reduces cognitive capacity. It's not about being poor; it's about experiencing poverty. The effect is detectable, consequential, and reversible.

The Evidence

Experimental Findings

From shopping malls in New Jersey to sugarcane fields in India — the data reveals the hidden cost of scarcity.

The New Jersey Mall Study

📍 New Jersey shopping mall
🧠 Raven's Matrices + Cognitive Control
📊 Median income split

Subjects were given hypothetical financial scenarios while completing classic cognitive tests. One group faced an easy problem ($150 car repair). The other faced a hard one ($1,500 repair).

For the rich, easy or hard problems made no difference. But for the poor, when the problem was challenging, their cognitive control and fluid intelligence dropped significantly.

The effect size was a Cohen's d of 0.9 — almost a full standard deviation. That's 13-14 IQ points. Enough to move someone from "average" to "mildly deficient" or from "average" to "gifted."

This effect is larger than the impact of losing an entire night's sleep. Simply being preoccupied by financial concerns made people substantially less intelligent in the moment.

The Indian Sugarcane Farmers

📍 Tamil Nadu, India
🔄 Same subjects, 4 months apart
🧠 Pre- vs Post-harvest

The ideal experiment would test the same person under scarcity and under abundance. Sugarcane farmers in India provided exactly this natural experiment.

Because harvest income arrives once a year and farmers fail to smooth consumption, they are poor before harvest and rich after. The same individuals were tested four months apart.

The same person scored lower, was slower, and made more errors before the harvest than after. Same individual. Same tests. Different financial circumstances. Different cognitive capacity.

This within-subject design eliminates all alternative explanations about education, culture, or innate ability. The effect is purely situational — and reversible.

Princeton Family Feud

📍 Princeton University lab
🎮 Family Feud game
⏱ Rich (50s) vs Poor (15s)

Princeton students were randomly assigned to be "rich" or "poor" in time while playing Family Feud. Rich students had 50 seconds per round. Poor students had only 15.

Some could borrow time at predatory rates (2 seconds repaid for every 1 borrowed). Others could not borrow at all.

The rich borrowed judiciously, distinguishing between no-interest and high-interest loans. The poor borrowed desperately, regardless of rate, ran out of time faster, completed fewer rounds, and earned fewer points.

These are Princeton students — not the "uneducated poor." Under scarcity, they replicated exactly the behaviors seen in payday loan borrowers: over-borrowing at predatory rates and hurting themselves.

The Dieters & Word Search

📍 California
🔍 Word search game
🍩 Food vs neutral words

Dieters and non-dieters played a word search game. Some grids contained food words (cake, donut); others contained only neutral words. Critically, researchers measured how long it took to find neutral words after seeing food words.

Non-dieters showed no difference. But dieters took significantly longer to find "tree" or "cloud" after seeing "donut" than after seeing "cloud."

Scarcity intrudes into what you do next. Food thoughts, once activated, distract from subsequent tasks. The dieter's mind is still processing the forbidden item while trying to perform.

This demonstrates how scarcity captures attention automatically and depletes the mental resources available for other tasks — even simple word finding.

The Minnesota Starvation Experiment

📍 University of Minnesota, 1943-44
👥 Conscientious objectors
🧠 Psychological effects of semi-starvation

During WWII, researchers starved healthy men to study how to refeed European populations. The physical effects were expected. The psychological effects were extraordinary.

The men spent their free time comparing food prices in newspapers, reading recipe books, and planning to become restaurateurs. Taken to movies to distract them, they reported noticing only the food.

"It was the worst time of my life, not because of the physical pain, but because all I could think about is food." — The scarcity instinct hijacks the mind completely.

This primitive, biological response makes evolutionary sense for a hungry mouse. But translated to modern life, it means the poor cannot help but focus on money, the busy on deadlines, the lonely on social rejection.

Implications

Designing for Scarcity

If we take bandwidth limits seriously, policy and design look very different.

✈️

The Cockpit Principle

WWII pilots kept retracting landing gear on landing. Psychologist Alphonse Chapanis didn't blame the pilots — he redesigned the cockpit. Levers for landing gear and flaps looked identical and sat next to each other. The solution was environmental, not personal.

📝

The FAFSA Form

Simply telling low-income students they were eligible for $6,000 in aid changed nothing. They were bandwidth-limited. But when researchers helped fill out the form with them, enrollment rose 30%. Reducing the bandwidth tax works.

⏰

Time Architecture

A foundation president has more obligations than most, but his meetings end with someone walking in five minutes early to say "Your meeting is ending." He doesn't run out of time because his environment is structured. Control beats raw capacity.

🛡️

Fault Tolerance

We design policies that are fault-intolerant: lifetime welfare limits with no flashing warnings. Small, honest mistakes have catastrophic consequences. Good design assumes error and minimizes its damage — just as cockpit design did for aviation safety.

Insights

Key Questions

Critical exchanges from the Q&A that illuminate the deeper implications of this research.

Is this about stress or about scarcity? +

There are two meanings of "stress." The biological process (cortisol, allostatic load) can actually increase IQ and working memory temporarily — the "fight or flight" harnessing of resources. But the colloquial meaning — having something on your mind that you cannot stop thinking about — is what scarcity creates. The key difference is that scarcity is double-sided: it makes you more productive on the immediate problem while making you worse at everything else. "Stress" obscures this duality.

What should someone with time scarcity do personally? +

You're not short on time. You're short on bandwidth. And bandwidth cannot be diced into units like time can. You can't control your bandwidth. The solution is to manage what occupies it. Sendhil's example: his phone fell in a toilet. During dinner without it, he had the best evening ever — not because friends got more interesting, but because no email could pull his bandwidth away. He now disables email on his phone. The insight: manage what pre-occupies you, not just your calendar.

How do we change the poverty narrative? +

The narrative must shift from pure personal responsibility to fault-tolerant design. In aviation, we don't just train pilots better — we redesign cockpits so that inevitable mistakes don't crash planes. Similarly, poverty policy should assume that even with the best intentions, people will make errors. The question is: does the system wash out small mistakes, or does it amplify them into catastrophe? Lifetime welfare limits without warnings are fault-intolerant. Good policy is forgiving architecture.

What about IQ and innate ability? +

Bandwidth captures both fluid intelligence (IQ) and executive control (self-control). Both move significantly under scarcity. The 13-14 IQ point drop is not about innate ability — the same Indian farmers scored differently before and after harvest. It's about mental resources being consumed by the immediate problem. Think of a computer: if web browsing is slow, it might not be a bad machine, but background processes consuming RAM. Scarcity runs background processes you cannot shut down.

Does this apply to the rich and busy too? +

Yes. The Family Feud experiment proves this. Princeton students with no real-world pressures, randomly made "time-poor" in a lab, replicated the exact behaviors of payday loan borrowers. When you remove social context, culture, and education and keep only scarcity, the psychology remains. This suggests scarcity is the active ingredient in the real world. The rich person who is time-scarce faces the same tunneling, the same trade-off thinking, the same temptation fatigue — just in a different domain.
