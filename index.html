A Journey Through Time

400 years of coffee heritage in Indonesia

1696

The Beginning

The Dutch East India Company introduces coffee to Indonesia, planting the first trees in Java. The seeds were smuggled from Yemen's port of Mocha, marking the start of Indonesia's coffee journey.

1830-1870

The Cultivation System

The Dutch enforced the Cultuurstelsel, requiring farmers to dedicate land to coffee. This exploitative system was later exposed by the novel "Max Havelaar," which helped change public opinion.

1876

Coffee Leaf Rust

A devastating outbreak of coffee leaf rust (Hemileia vastatrix) swept through Indonesia, wiping out most Arabica Typica cultivars. This led to the introduction of Robusta coffee.

1945

Independence

Indonesia gained independence from the Dutch. The colonial plantations were nationalized, and today over 90% of coffee is grown by smallholder farmers on farms averaging just 1 hectare.

2025/26

Modern Era

Indonesia is now the world's 4th largest coffee producer, with 12.5 million 60-kg bags produced annually. The specialty coffee market is thriving, with unique varieties and processing methods gaining global recognition.

Sumatra

Key Areas: Aceh (Gayo), Lake Toba (Lintong, Mandheling)

Altitude: 1,100 - 1,600 masl

Processing: Wet-hulled (Giling Basah)

Flavor Profile: Full body, low acidity, earthy, dark chocolate, herbal notes, sometimes spicy. The wet-hulling process creates the signature syrupy body and complex earthy flavors.

Notable: Produces 70-75% of Indonesia's total coffee output. Mandheling is named after the Mandailing people, not a place.

Java

Key Areas: Ijen Plateau, Preanger

Altitude: 1,400+ masl

Processing: Washed, some aged (Old Java)

Flavor Profile: Clean, bright acidity, herbal undertones, spicy notes, sweet finish. Aged Java develops cedar and cinnamon notes with heavy body.

Notable: Birthplace of Indonesian coffee (1696). Famous for "Mocha Java" blend. Five historic Dutch estates still operate: Blawan, Jampit, Pancoer, Kayumas, and Tugosari.

Bali

Key Areas: Kintamani Highlands

Altitude: 1,200 - 1,600 masl

Processing: Washed, Natural, Honey

Flavor Profile: Clean cup, citrus (lemon/orange), floral, milk chocolate, mild intensity. Higher acidity than Sumatra.

Notable: Farmers use the "Subak Abian" system based on Hindu philosophy "Tri Hita Karana." Indonesia's first Geographic Indication (G.I.) was issued for Kintamani coffee in 2008. Almost all exported to Japan.

Sulawesi (Toraja)

Key Areas: Tana Toraja Highlands, Kalosi

Altitude: 1,400 - 2,000 masl

Processing: Wet-hulled

Flavor Profile: Full-bodied, rich, earthy, sweet spice, dark chocolate, ripe fruit undertones, vibrant yet low-toned acidity.

Notable: 95% Arabica in Toraja. S795 (Jember) variety is common - one of the earliest leaf rust resistant varieties. Mainly exported to Japan by Key Coffee.

Flores

Key Areas: Bajawa

Altitude: 1,200 - 1,800 masl

Processing: Washed, Pulped Natural

Flavor Profile: Sweet, smooth, milk chocolate, cocoa, citrus lift, clean finish, syrupy body.

Notable: Relatively new to specialty coffee. Volcanic Andosols soil is extremely fertile. 12 cooperatives work together to bring coffee to market.

Papua

Key Areas: Baliem Valley

Altitude: 1,400 - 2,000 masl

Processing: Washed

Flavor Profile: Clean, complex, sweet, fruity, floral notes with good acidity.

Notable: One of the newest coffee regions in Indonesia. Gaining recognition for high-quality Arabica with unique profiles due to isolated growing conditions.

Where It All Began

Balanced Bright Acidity Herbal Spicy Clean

Process: Washed, some aged (Old Java)

Best for: Pour-over, Drip

The Hidden Jewel

Grown in the Kintamani Highlands between volcanoes. Known for organic farming and unique Subak Abian system.

Toraja Treasure

From the highlands of Tana Toraja. Multi-dimensional coffee with 95% Arabica production in the region.

Process: Wet-hulled

Best for: Chemex, French Press

The Rising Star

Sweet Milk Chocolate Citrus Smooth Syrupy

Named "Flowers" by Portuguese explorers. New to specialty coffee but gaining rapid popularity for quality.

Process: Washed, Pulped Natural

Harvest: May - October

The Middle Ground

The skin and some pulp are removed, but the sticky mucilage ("honey") is left on the bean during drying. The amount of mucilage determines the "color" (white, yellow, red, black).

Coffee Varieties

The genetic diversity of Indonesian coffee

Typica

The Original

The first variety introduced by the Dutch in the 1600s. Most was wiped out by leaf rust in the 1880s, but some survives in Sumatra (Bergandal, Sidikalang).

Sweet Clean Complex

Tim Tim (HDT)

The Survivor

Natural Arabica-Robusta hybrid from Timor. Highly disease resistant and widely planted across Indonesia. Parent of Catimor and Sarchimor.

Bold Thick Body Resilient

S-795 (Jember)

The Sweet One

Developed in India from Bourbon lineage. One of the earliest leaf rust resistant varieties. Known for sweet, maple syrup or brown sugar notes.

Sweet Maple Brown Sugar

USDA 762

The Ethiopian Connection

Introduced from Ethiopia in the 1950s. From the same region as Geisha but adapted differently in Indonesia. Found mainly in Java and Sumatra.

Mild Subtle Historical

Ateng (Catimor)

The Workhorse

Local name for Catimor lines. High yielding and disease resistant but often criticized for cup quality. Some locally adapted types show excellent characteristics.

High Yield Disease Resistant Variable Quality

Gayo 1

The Local Star

Local adaptation of Tim Tim in the Gayo highlands of Aceh. Well-known across Indonesia for its quality and adaptation to local conditions.

Local Adapted Quality Aceh Special
